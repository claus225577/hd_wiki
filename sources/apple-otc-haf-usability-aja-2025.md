# Usability and Performance of the Apple Over-the-Counter Hearing Aid Feature

## Summary

First independent (non-Apple) academic evaluation of Apple's Over-the-Counter Hearing Aid Feature (OTC-HAF) shipped with AirPods Pro 2. Examines usability and laboratory performance in adults with self-perceived mild-to-moderate hearing loss. Establishes that the consumer-electronics OTC self-fit pathway meets a credible clinical-utility bar without an audiologist in the loop.

## Key Extractions

- **Cohort:** Adults reporting self-perceived mild-to-moderate hearing loss.
- **Methods:** Combined the validated mHealth App Usability Questionnaire (MAUQ) with a study-specific questionnaire (self-rated sound quality, ease of use, satisfaction) and laboratory measurements of the personalized amplification.
- **Usability (MAUQ):** Mean score **6.7 / 7** — at the high end of the scale.
- **Subjective ratings:** High satisfaction, good sound quality, ease of use reported.
- **Laboratory performance:** Evaluated against expected hearing-aid prescription targets (specific gain/output details in the paper).

## Why It Matters

1. **Independent validation.** Until now most data on the AirPods OTC-HAF was Apple-generated. An academic AJA paper closes that gap with a credible methodology.
2. **Self-fit pathway is real.** The clinical-utility threshold for an OTC hearing aid is roughly "as good as a basic clinic-fit device for mild-to-moderate loss without doing harm." This study supports that claim for the AirPods pathway.
3. **OTC market acceleration.** Combined with Apple's geographic rollout (see [[airpods-hearing-aid-country-expansion-may-2026]]), independent clinical evidence removes a barrier audiologists used to cite.
4. **Research signal.** Suggests the next wave of OTC validation papers should focus on real-world outcomes (intelligibility in noise, daily use compliance) rather than just lab usability.

## Caveats / Open Questions

- Cohort is self-perceived hearing loss — not audiometrically classified.
- Lab measurement of personalized amplification is not equivalent to real-world functional outcomes; long-term retention and EHF (extended-high-frequency) performance not addressed.
- Study does not benchmark against premium prescription RIC devices — only against expected OTC prescription targets.
