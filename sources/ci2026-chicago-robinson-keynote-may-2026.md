# CI2026 Chicago — James Robinson Keynote

## Key Facts

- **Conference:** CI2026 Chicago, the 2026 Conference on Cochlear Implants
- **Organizer:** American Cochlear Implant (ACI) Alliance
- **Dates:** May 7–9, 2026
- **Venue:** Hyatt Regency Chicago, Chicago, IL
- **Headline Speaker:** Dr. James A. Robinson, 2024 Nobel Laureate in Economic Sciences
- **Robinson's relevant work:** Co-author of "Why Nations Fail" (with Daron Acemoglu); 2024 Nobel Prize shared with Acemoglu and Simon Johnson "for studies of how institutions are formed and affect prosperity"
- **Audience:** Audiologists, otologic surgeons, speech-language pathologists, psychologists, scientists, engineers, educators, students, advocates

## Why This Is Notable

Inviting an institutional economist — not a clinician, not a chip designer, not an AI researcher — as the headline speaker at a cochlear implant conference reframes the field's central problem. The diagnostic / surgical / signal-processing edges of CI care have advanced dramatically; the access edge has not. Robinson's career-long argument is that **institutions, not technology, determine which populations capture the gains from innovation**.

The choice of keynote signals that the CI field is publicly acknowledging that:
1. CI utilization rates remain low (estimated ~5–10% of adult candidates in the US receive an implant) despite strong outcomes evidence
2. Geographic, financial, and reimbursement barriers — not device limitations — are the binding constraints for most candidates
3. The next decade of CI growth is more about scaling access systems than about scaling technology

## Implications for Data Science / AI in Hearing Care

- **Modeling the system, not just the device:** Outcome data is well-developed; access pathway data (referral chains, time-to-implant, drop-off points) is much weaker. There is significant ML opportunity in mapping where candidates leak out of the pipeline.
- **Health-equity ML:** Predictive models for identifying CI candidates currently underserved (rural, lower-income, racial/ethnic minorities) could be built on existing audiology + EHR data, but need institutional buy-in to act on.
- **Cross-country comparisons:** Robinson's framework points toward comparative analysis — why do some healthcare systems (Australia, parts of Northern Europe) have CI utilization 3–5x higher than others? Useful natural experiments for evidence generation.
- **Instrumentation of the workforce gap:** The audiologist/CI-surgeon shortage interacts with reimbursement and referral patterns; data-science methods (queueing models, optimization, causal inference) are well-suited to surfacing where institutional changes would yield the highest ROI in CI access.
