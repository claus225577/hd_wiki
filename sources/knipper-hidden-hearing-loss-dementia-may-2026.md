# Hidden Hearing Loss as a Brain Burden — Knipper, May 2026

## Key Extractions

### Statement
- Tübingen-based hearing researcher **Prof. Dr. Marlies Knipper** publicly emphasized (1 May 2026) that standard audiometric tests overlook a critical frequency and suprathreshold range where cognitive risks first become apparent.
- The clinical takeaway she pushes: **hearing health is a factor in cognitive performance**, not merely a comfort issue in old age.

### What "Hidden Hearing Loss" Means Here
- Refers to suprathreshold deficits — auditory nerve / synaptopathy damage — that don't show up on a routine pure-tone audiogram.
- Affected listeners often report "I can hear, but I can't understand," especially in noise.
- Knipper's lab has long argued these deficits are mechanistically linked to early cognitive load and decline.

## Relevance
- Reinforces the critique that the **pure-tone audiogram is an under-spec'd outcome measure** for hearing healthcare decisions and AI model targets.
- Aligns with the Apple Hearing Study finding (May 2026) that 16% of clinically normal participants self-rate fair/poor hearing — likely a population-scale signature of hidden hearing loss.
- Strengthens the case for adopting speech-in-noise testing, extended-high-frequency audiometry, and self-reported hearing as additional outcome measures in hearing aid validation and ML training pipelines.
- Connects to the broader hearing loss → dementia evidence chain (Lancet Commission, ACHIEVE, Framingham).
