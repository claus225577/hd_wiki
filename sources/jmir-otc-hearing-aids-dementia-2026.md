# JMIR Human Factors 2026;13:e83857 — OTC Hearing Aids in People with Dementia

## Key Facts

- **Venue:** JMIR Human Factors (2026, vol. 13, article e83857)
- **Design:** Semi-structured qualitative interview study
- **Population:** People with dementia (and their care partners) who have used or are candidates for OTC hearing aids
- **Goal:** Identify facilitators and barriers to OTC HA adoption / continued use in this population

## Why It Matters

The 2022 FDA OTC rule was framed as an accessibility win for mild-to-moderate hearing loss — but most efficacy and usability data has been collected from cognitively healthy older adults. The population most affected by the **hearing-loss → cognitive-decline pathway** has been systematically underrepresented in OTC trials.

This paper begins to close that gap with a qualitative human-factors lens:
- What goes wrong with self-fitting and self-management when the user has dementia
- Which features and care-partner workflows make OTC HAs usable in this population
- Where the OTC model breaks (vs. where it works) compared to prescription / clinical fitting

## Why This Pairs with Today's WCA Story

The Pichora-Fuller award lecture at WCA 2026 ("Hearing and Healthy Aging: The Imperative for Communication Accessibility") explicitly argues that audiology has been measuring the wrong variables — that the cognitive-load × hearing-loss interaction matters more than audiogram-level outcomes alone. This JMIR paper is a concrete empirical example of what changes when you take that frame seriously: you ask different questions about OTC HAs (do care-partners reload batteries? does the app onboarding succeed? do users remember to wear them?) and you get answers the standard OTC literature doesn't have.
