# WCA 2026 Seoul Day 3 AI Panel — Edwards, Launer, Strophal on AI Consent, Marketing, and Clinical Surface

## Bibliographic
- **Reporter:** Liam O'Dell (independent journalist; congress floor write-up)
- **Date filed:** 2026-05-26
- **Venue:** World Congress of Audiology (WCA) 2026, Seoul — Day 3
- **Format:** Panel discussion, full session reportage

## Key Claims (verbatim where quoted)

### Brent Edwards — National Acoustic Laboratories, Australia
- **Headline claim:** Audiologists do *not* require explicit patient consent to use AI tools in clinical workflow. Frames AI as a tool comparable to a computer or reference material.
- **Direct quote:** "You don't need a contract, don't need permission, in my opinion."
- **Caveat (also direct):** "Be open about it, don't hide it." — i.e., transparency is non-negotiable even if formal consent is not.
- **Trust framing (two barriers):**
  1. *Audiologist trust* in AI capability (will the model do what the package insert claims).
  2. *Patient trust* that the recommendation/advice originated from a trained professional rather than a machine.
- **Direct quote on trust risk:** "If you think you're getting advice from a computer instead of a trained professional, you're going to lose the trust."

### Stefan Launer — Sonova, Switzerland
- **Headline claim:** The industry's AI conversation is mis-anchored to capability and feature-marketing rather than to outcomes.
- **Direct quote:** "Stop talking about AI in hearing care, let's start talking about what is the benefit."
- **Implication:** Sonova-internal signal that the next product narrative cycle should be benefit-claim led, not chip-architecture or DNN-architecture led — relevant to interpretation of Sonova's recent comms cadence (FY 2025/26 results May 18; Phonak new platform teaser for 2026).

### Maren Strophal — Switzerland (affiliation not disclosed in source)
- **Three-bucket clinical-surface taxonomy** for where AI supports hearing care:
  1. Clinical efficiency and back-office / business operations.
  2. Patient education and knowledge transfer.
  3. Pre- and post-appointment counseling and support.
- **What this taxonomy *excludes*:** in-fitting personalization and DNN-in-the-aid sound processing — i.e., Strophal is mapping the *clinic-side* AI surface, not the *device-side* surface. Useful disambiguation; most public AI-in-hearing discussion conflates the two.

## Why This Matters

### Genuinely new contribution to the wiki
The clinician-side AI consent question has not been a tracked theme in this wiki to date. Two existing concept pages graze it (`agentic-engineering-hearing-rd.md` covers AI tooling in R&D; `eu-ai-act-medical-devices.md` covers regulatory framing) but neither addresses the question of *whether a clinician using AI in a consult must disclose or seek consent from the patient*. This is the first wiki source to anchor that question to a named, on-the-record claim.

### Editorial signal from Sonova
Launer's "stop talking about AI, talk about benefit" line is the second 2026 instance of Sonova senior leadership steering the narrative away from feature-led AI marketing. It pairs with the May 18 FY 2025/26 results commentary that emphasized growth in Audiological Care alongside Hearing Instruments — both signal a shift from product-feature anchoring to patient-outcome anchoring.

### Methodological gap surfaced
Strophal's three-bucket taxonomy makes explicit a partition the wiki has been implicitly conflating. Worth surfacing in any future synthesis on "AI surface area in hearing care" — the in-aid DNN literature and the clinic-side LLM literature should be tracked separately because they have different deployment, regulatory, and consent profiles.

## Open Questions
- Does the EU AI Act's risk-classification framework actually require informed consent for clinical AI use, or only transparency? Edwards' "no consent needed" claim is jurisdiction-specific and may not survive a Class IIa medical-device classification in EU markets.
- Is Sonova's "benefit not features" messaging shift defensive (a response to OTC / Apple competitive pressure) or offensive (preparing for a clinical-outcomes-led product launch)?
- Where do clinic-side LLM tools sit in the FDA's SaMD framework once they affect dispensing recommendations?
