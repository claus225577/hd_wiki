# EAN Congress 2026 — Hearing Aid Use, Epilepsy, and Dementia Risk (27 Jun 2026)

## Summary

Presented at the **European Academy of Neurology (EAN) Congress** on **27 Jun 2026**: adults with both **epilepsy** and **hearing loss** who use hearing aids show a **23% lower dementia risk** compared with peers who have the same comorbidities but do not use hearing aids.

This is the first widely reported comorbidity-stratified estimate of the hearing-aid → dementia-risk effect where **both** conditions are neurological. Prior high-profile evidence (ACHIEVE 2023, Framingham 2025, Apple Hearing Study 2026, medRxiv plasma-biomarker preprint Jun 2026) has focused on either the general population or hearing-loss-only cohorts.

## Headline Result

- **Population:** adults with epilepsy + hearing loss
- **Comparison:** hearing-aid users vs non-users within that comorbid population
- **Effect size:** ~23% lower dementia incidence for hearing-aid users
- **Setting:** presented at EAN 2026 (Europe's largest neurology congress)

## Why It Matters

1. **Comorbidity subgroups can carry larger absolute benefits.** Effect sizes derived from general hearing-loss cohorts (Lancet 2024 modifiable-risk-factor ~7%, Framingham under-70 sub-cohort 61%) are averages. Two-hit populations (epilepsy + hearing loss) still show a materially large effect — plausibly because both cognitive-load and social-isolation mechanisms are amplified when a seizure disorder is layered on top of sensory deprivation.

2. **Reframes hearing aids as a neurology-adjacent intervention.** The venue itself (a neurology congress) signals that the "hearing-aids-as-cognitive-intervention" narrative is now being taken seriously *outside* audiology.

3. **Missing datasets are the bottleneck.** Answering "who benefits most cognitively?" at population scale requires **linked audiogram + neurological + demographic + longitudinal outcome** data. This is the same data-infrastructure gap flagged by the Frontiers in Dementia review (May 2026) and by the Apple Hearing Study (May 2026).

## Methodological Notes / Caveats

- Available reporting is a **press release** summarizing a congress presentation, not the peer-reviewed manuscript. Effect-size interpretation should be treated as provisional until the full paper indexes.
- Directionality is not established as causal in this press summary — an observational comorbidity-stratified analysis cannot rule out that epilepsy patients who reach for hearing aids differ systematically (adherence, access, health engagement) from those who don't.
- Baseline dementia risk in the epilepsy + hearing-loss population is substantially elevated; the 23% relative risk reduction may correspond to a large absolute risk reduction.

## Implications for Hearing-AI R&D

- **Outcome-metric design:** cognitive trajectory belongs in the hearing-aid validation stack alongside intelligibility (see [[../wiki/concepts/hearing-loss-dementia-link]] Frontiers-review commentary).
- **Personalization targets:** ML personalization should be trained not only on acoustic-fit outcomes but on **who benefits most cognitively** — comorbidity features (epilepsy, cardiovascular, metabolic) are candidate covariates.
- **Trial-design implication:** comorbidity-stratified sub-analyses are worth pre-registering in future hearing-intervention trials; the ACHIEVE-style single-population design leaves the strongest responders undetected.
