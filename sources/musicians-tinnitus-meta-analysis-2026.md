# Meta-Analysis: 42.6% of Musicians Experience Tinnitus

## Source Metadata
- **Venue:** Otolaryngology–Head and Neck Surgery
- **Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **N:** 67 studies, 28,000+ musicians, 21 countries
- **Industry coverage surfaced:** Feb–Mar 2026; re-surfaced May 2026 in Neuroscience News / Medical Xpress / Classic FM
- **Ingested:** 2026-05-17

## Key Statistics
| Condition | Musicians | Controls | Relative Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tinnitus | **42.6%** | 13.2% | ~3.2× |
| Hearing loss | 25.7% | 11.6% | ~2.2× |
| Hyperacusis | 37.3% | 15.3% | ~2.4× |

Among musicians with tinnitus: 76.3% occasional, 15.6% permanent.

## Notable Finding: Genre Neutrality
**No significant difference in prevalence between classical and rock/pop musicians.** This contradicts conventional wisdom that genre exposure profile is the dominant risk factor. The study argues that **instrument type, ensemble seating position, room acoustics, and protective-equipment attitudes** are more critical than genre alone.

## Why It Matters
- Quantifies the occupational-hearing-loss target population for tinnitus digital therapeutics and custom in-ear monitor / in-ear protection products.
- The "44% of musicians" headline is now sourced and citable; expect it in industry marketing for tinnitus-focused DTx products (SilentCloud / Lenire / Neuromod / Oto), custom IEM vendors (Ultimate Ears / Sensaphonics), and OEM positioning for premium musician-tier hearing protection.
- The genre-neutrality finding shifts intervention design from "warn classical musicians less, rock musicians more" to **"intervene on seating / acoustics / protection-adherence regardless of genre."**
- Suggests a measurable cohort for longitudinal phenotyping: musicians have known high-exposure, known instrumented work environments, and high adherence to wearable tracking — almost an ideal real-world-data population.
