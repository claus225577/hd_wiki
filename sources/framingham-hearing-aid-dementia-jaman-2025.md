# Framingham Hearing Aid → Dementia Risk (JAMA Neurology Letters, Aug 2025)

## Summary

Observational analysis from the Framingham Heart Study comparing dementia risk across three groups: untreated hearing loss, treated hearing loss (with aids), and no hearing loss. Publication in the Letters section of JAMA Neurology (Aug 18, 2025). The study **resurfaced in the May 2026 news cycle** via International Hearing Society's Better Hearing Month PR (May 5, 2026) and McKnight's coverage (May 6, 2026).

## Key Numbers

- **N:** 2,953 adults aged 60+
- **Cohorts:** Two Framingham generations, standardized hearing tests 1977–1979 or 1995–1998
- **Follow-up:** Up to 20 years
- **Dementia incidence:** ~20% of participants developed dementia
- **Under-70 stratum:** Hearing-aid users had **61% lower** incident-dementia risk vs untreated peers
- **Under-70 stratum (no hearing loss):** **29% lower** risk vs untreated peers (reference)
- **70+ stratum:** No statistically significant association between hearing aid use and dementia

## The Age Cutoff

The **70-year threshold is the headline finding, not the 61% number alone.** The age-stratified result has two interpretations, both consequential:

1. **Biological window:** Cognitive-decline trajectories diverge in midlife (50–69); by 70+, the relevant neuropathology may already be entrenched and hearing intervention provides little protective return.
2. **Selection / dose:** People who get hearing aids before 70 may have wider longitudinal "treated wear-time exposure" — the effect is dose-dependent rather than age-dependent strictly.

Either reading points to **earlier intervention** as the design imperative.

## Significance

- Reinforces the Lancet Commission framing of hearing loss as the largest modifiable mid-life dementia risk factor
- Adds **age stratification** as a specific clinical and product-design parameter
- For the hearing-aid industry: forces a re-examination of phenotype assumptions (the 75+ wearer being the default) — the most consequential intervention cohort is 50–69 and currently underserved by device design, retail channels, and brand positioning

## Methodological Caveats

- **Observational, not RCT** — cannot rule out healthy-user bias (people who choose hearing aids may have lower baseline dementia risk for unobserved reasons)
- Hearing aid use is **self-reported** — no telemetry / wear-time validation
- Framingham cohort is predominantly white US population — generalizability to other populations is limited
- Letters-section format limits methodological detail vs full paper

## Relationship to ACHIEVE

Different evidence type:
- **ACHIEVE (2023):** RCT, 977 adults 70–84, 3-yr follow-up → 48% slowing of cognitive decline rate in high-risk subgroup
- **Francis/Seshadri (2025):** Observational cohort, 2,953 adults 60+, 20-yr follow-up → 61% lower incident-dementia risk in under-70 stratum

The Framingham analysis is consistent with ACHIEVE in spirit (treatment helps cognition) but extends the effect to incident dementia (not just decline rate) and identifies an age-stratified pattern ACHIEVE was not powered to detect.
