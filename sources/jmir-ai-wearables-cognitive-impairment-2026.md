# AI and Wearables for Early Detection of Cognitive Impairment and Dementia: Systematic Review

Published Feb 23, 2026 in the Journal of Medical Internet Research (JMIR), article ID e86262. Authors: Ander Cejudo, Markel Arrojo, Cristina Martín, Aitor Almeida (Vicomtech Foundation, University of Deusto, Biodonostia Health Research Institute).

## Key Extractions

- **Scope:** Systematic review synthesizing 49 studies that pair wearable devices with artificial intelligence for early detection of cognitive impairment and dementia.
- **Framing shift:** From descriptive associations between wearable signals and cognition toward a **digital phenotyping framework** — passive, continuous behavioral and physiological signals as preclinical biomarkers.
- **Window of interest:** Preclinical — i.e., before clinical diagnosis is feasible by conventional means. AI-driven prediction is positioned as the lever that makes earlier-than-clinical detection scalable.
- **Signal categories surveyed:**
  - Activity / physical mobility (gait, step count, walking speed)
  - Sleep architecture and continuity
  - Heart-rate variability and autonomic markers
  - Behavioral patterns (routine deviation, social engagement)
- **Methodology gap identified:** Heterogeneous data formats, small sample sizes, limited longitudinal follow-up, and lack of standardized AI evaluation protocols are the field's main barriers to clinical translation.
- **Ear-based wearables noted:** The review acknowledges hearing aids and smart earphones as a distinct emerging substrate for ecological data collection — currently underexplored relative to wrist-based devices.

## Relevance to Hearing AI

- Establishes a peer-reviewed evidence base (49 studies, Feb 2026) for the broader thesis that **wearables + AI = preclinical cognitive screening**.
- Hearing aids are arguably the most underused substrate in this framework: longest daily wear time, most consistent population coverage among older adults, already capturing acoustic environment + conversational uptake, and adding biometric sensors (PPG, motion, temperature) in current and next-generation devices.
- Bridges the [[../wiki/concepts/hearing-loss-dementia-link]] (epidemiology / RCT evidence) with [[../wiki/concepts/longitudinal-hearing-phenotyping]] (measurement framework).
- Implies a clear data-science roadmap for hearing-aid manufacturers: define the minimum-viable digital-phenotype feature set extractable from existing telemetry, validate against clinical cognitive endpoints, and treat dementia screening as a derived product of routine device use rather than a separate clinical workflow.
