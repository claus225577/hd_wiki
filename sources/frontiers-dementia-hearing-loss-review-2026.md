# Frontiers in Dementia — Dementia and Hearing Loss Review (2026)

## Bibliographic
- **Authors:** Multi-author review
- **Published:** May 2026
- **Venue:** Frontiers in Dementia, vol/article 10.3389/frdem.2026.1736003
- **URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/dementia/articles/10.3389/frdem.2026.1736003/full

## What
A comprehensive review article consolidating the body of research linking hearing loss with cognitive decline and dementia risk. Covers:
- Observational cohorts (UK Biobank, ACHIEVE, others)
- Mechanism candidates: sensory deprivation/cognitive load, common cause (vascular, neurodegenerative), social isolation pathway
- Hearing-aid intervention trials and the gap between "reduces dementia incidence" and "improves memory on standardized tests"
- Open methodological questions: causality, confounding by socioeconomic status, reverse-causality concerns, biomarkers

## Key Findings (per the review's summary)
- Evidence linking hearing loss to elevated dementia risk continues to accumulate across diverse populations.
- Hearing-aid use is associated with **lower dementia incidence** in observational cohorts (recent example: 5% vs 8% dementia incidence at follow-up among prescribed vs not-prescribed users), but cognitive-test scores (memory, processing speed) typically show small, non-significant gains.
- **Mechanism remains unresolved** — the review explicitly underscores that key questions about how, why, and when these conditions are linked remain open.
- Calls for biomarkers (e.g., FSR — frequency-specific response ratio derived from MRI/EEG) to identify high-risk subgroups and stratify intervention studies.

## Why It Matters
This is the kind of review that gets cited at HLAA, AAA, and public-health policy panels for the next 12 months. For the hearing-AI ecosystem, the operational takeaway is structural:

1. The **outcome measure** for hearing intervention is shifting from "dB gain restored" toward "dementia/cognitive decline averted" — which has fundamental implications for clinical-trial design, for hearing-aid telemetry (we now need longitudinal cognitive proxy signals), and for the FUEL framework / listening-effort metrics already tracked in the wiki.
2. The "intervention works for dementia but not memory tests" pattern points to **wrong outcome variable, not weak treatment** — consistent with the wiki's existing thesis on the subjective–objective hearing gap.
3. The mechanism uncertainty is the opening for ML: digital phenotyping via hearing aids (already a wiki concept) is one of the few longitudinal at-scale data streams that could disentangle sensory-deprivation vs common-cause hypotheses.
