## Key Facts

- JAMA 2025 data: 75% of US counties lack sufficient audiology providers, creating a severe access gap particularly in rural and low-income areas.
- Teleaudiology fills the gap by enabling remote hearing evaluations (audiometry), hearing aid fittings, follow-up adjustments, and counseling via video and connected hearing aid apps.
- Services offered remotely include: pure-tone audiometry (via validated home testing), real-ear measurement guidance, hearing aid programming, and tinnitus counseling.
- Limitations: teleaudiology is less suitable for advanced hearing loss requiring complex in-person fitting, custom earmold impressions, or surgical candidacy evaluation.
- Growth driven by hearing aid manufacturer remote fine-tuning apps (Phonak myPhonak, ReSound Smart 3D, Oticon Companion) enabling audiologist-to-patient remote programming.
- Regulatory frameworks still evolving — reimbursement parity for teleaudiology services varies by state and payer.
- Access gap intersects with OTC market growth: patients without nearby audiologists increasingly turning to OTC as first resort.
