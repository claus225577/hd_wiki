# WHO Hearing Programs

World Health Organization (WHO) programs and initiatives focused on hearing health globally. The WHO's hearing work spans advocacy, data, direct grants, and coordination across health systems, schools, and communities.

## World Hearing Day (March 3, Annually)

Annual global campaign to raise awareness and promote hearing care:
- **Date**: March 3 each year
- **2025 theme**: "Changing Mindsets" — addressing stigma and misconceptions about hearing loss and hearing aids
- **2026 theme**: "From communities to classrooms: hearing care for all children" — focus on childhood hearing loss and educational settings

The campaign produces materials for use by audiologists, health ministries, NGOs, and media globally.

## Scale of the Problem

WHO data on hearing loss burden:
- **90 million children and adolescents** globally are affected by hearing loss
- **60%+ of childhood hearing loss is preventable** — through immunization, maternal care, noise reduction, and early screening
- Hearing loss is the most common sensory disability globally
- Unaddressed hearing loss in childhood causes language delays, educational underachievement, and social exclusion

## Key Programs & Initiatives

### World Hearing Forum
- Multi-stakeholder platform convening governments, civil society, health systems, and industry
- Tracks national progress toward hearing care integration in health systems
- Advocates for inclusion of hearing health in Universal Health Coverage (UHC) frameworks

### Small Grants Program
- **Grant size: $800 USD**
- Awarded to organizations and individuals running community-level hearing awareness projects
- Intentionally small to reach grassroots/local organizations in low- and middle-income countries
- Examples: school hearing screenings, community awareness campaigns, training community health workers

### School Hearing Screening Advocacy
- WHO advocates for universal hearing screening programs in schools
- Rationale: 60%+ preventable childhood hearing loss means early detection enables intervention before language/learning damage occurs
- Cross-sectoral approach: health ministry + education ministry coordination required
- Partners with ENT/audiology professional societies and NGOs to implement programs

### Cross-Sectoral Partnerships
- WHO hearing programs explicitly require coordination across:
  - **Health systems** (audiologists, ENT physicians, primary care)
  - **Education systems** (teachers, school administrators, special education)
  - **Community organizations** (NGOs, disability rights groups)
  - **Industry** (hearing aid manufacturers, telehealth providers)
- This cross-sectoral framing reflects the reality that most hearing loss interventions happen outside clinical settings

## WHO Hearing Loss Data & Reports
- WHO "World Report on Hearing" (2021) — comprehensive global analysis
- Publishes burden-of-disease estimates, cost of unaddressed hearing loss, and return on investment for hearing care
- Estimate: unaddressed hearing loss costs $980 billion USD globally per year (direct + indirect costs)

## Relevance to Hearing + AI Space
- WHO programs create demand signals for low-cost, scalable hearing solutions — exactly where OTC hearing aids (Apple AirPods Pro 2, etc.) and telehealth audiology tools play
- School screening advocacy opens a market for consumer hearing test tools
- Small Grants Program ($800) highlights that most impactful interventions are non-clinical and community-based — different from where most hearing AI R&D investment flows
- WHO's 60% preventable framing emphasizes upstream prevention (noise, infection, maternal health) vs. downstream treatment — a tension with the hearing aid industry's device-centric approach
